Biography of Alexander the Great

From Macedonian prince to world conqueror.

Born to King Philip II of Macedon and Queen Olympias, Alexander’s early life was marked by political instability and ambition. From a young age, he was trained in the arts of war and leadership. His mother, Olympias, was fiercely protective of him, and his father, Philip, ensured that Alexander received the best education possible, including tutoring by Aristotle, one of the greatest minds of the ancient world.

At the age of 20, following the assassination of his father, Alexander ascended to the throne. His reign was immediately challenged by internal revolts and external threats, but Alexander quickly demonstrated his ability to lead. He suppressed uprisings and set his sights on the Persian Empire, his father’s long-standing enemy.

Born in 356 BCE, Alexander was tutored by Aristotle and trained in warfare by his father, King Philip II of Macedon.

After becoming king in 336 BCE, he embarked on a legendary campaign, defeating the Persian Empire and expanding Greek culture. Alexander’s early successes were instrumental in establishing his reputation as a military genius. His first major victory, the Battle of Granicus in 334 BCE, marked the beginning of his campaign against the Persians. Over the next several years, Alexander would go on to defeat Darius III and conquer much of the Persian Empire, including the vital cities of Babylon, Susa, and Persepolis. His bold strategy and tactical brilliance allowed him to win battle after battle, often against forces much larger than his own.

Though his life was short—he died in 323 BCE at the age of 32—his impact on the world was immeasurable. Alexander’s ability to blend cultures, his military innovations, and his ambition to create a unified world left a lasting imprint on history. Today, he is remembered as one of the greatest leaders in the history of warfare and governance.
